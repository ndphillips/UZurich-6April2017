Asking for data

Towards the start of my PhD, I requested data from someone. Here was the response:

What He wrote:

"This data is pretty old and it would take me a while to find it. If you can collaborate with a more senior researcher, and have a specific research question, I might be able to locate it."

What I understood:

"My data are unorganized and it would take me a long time to prepare and distribute it to someone without getting something in return."

The Problem

  • For many researchers, while the publication is important to share, the data and analyses details are not.

Progress is being made

  • Journals are asking for raw data.
  • Open Science Framework (OSF)
  • However, sharing the data is just the start: it must be documented, and in a useable format.

A solution: Reasearch as an R package

  • Store everything in an R package: Data, data descriptions, analyses, tutorials.

  • Share the package at the time of publication (before someone asks for it)
  • Now everyone (even your future self) can always recover the data and analyses.

Making good decisions

Fast and Frugal Trees (FFTs)

  • Decision trees are simple, sequential, rule-based decision algorithms.
  • A fast and frugal decision tree (FFT) is the simplest possible decision tree (Martignon et al., 2008).
  • FFTs have been used to predict depression (Jenny et al., 2013), terrorist attacks (Garcia, 2016), and bank failure (Aikman et al., 2014; Neth et al., 2014)

Neth et al. (2014). "Homo heuristicus in the financial world".

Predicting patient success

  • Data set: Baden Wuerttemberg dataset -- 1101 patients, 46 cues (age, sex, diagnosis, drug history, etc...)
  • Goal: Predict who will be successfully released.
  • Problem: There was no off-the-shelf method to construct FFTs.
  • Solution: FFTrees, an easy-to-use R package that constructs, visualizes, and implements FFTs from any data.

Phillips et al. (under review). FFTrees: An R package to create, visualise, and impliment fast and frugal decision trees

FFTrees

# Step 0: Install and load FFTrees
install.packages("FFTrees")
library("FFTrees")

# Step 1: Create the trees
patient.fft <- FFTrees(formula = fds.63.crit ~., 
                     data = fds.63.data)

# Step 2: View summary statistics
print(patient.fft)

# Step 3: Visualise the tree
plot(patient.fft)
